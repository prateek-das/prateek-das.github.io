Stigmas Among African-Americans

Among African-Americans, mental health issues and stigmas can be assumed to emerge from the horrors and tragedies from the dark trans-Atlantic slave trade, where millions of native Africans were forcibly removed and dislocated from their tribal homes by European and American colonizers. These same men, who were kidnapped from their families, were boarded onto large ships, where they were chained and kept in living conditions unimaginable, where every square foot was covered by a person or two, making the ships overpopulated and disease on the rise.

When they arrived on the shores of the European, imperialist countries, they were turned into slaves and given extreme work and labor to carry out. They were tortured and beaten when they didn't comply, and had their rights stripped of. In short, trauma can be debated as a cause of their views on mental health.

However, there are a few main points that we can take away from the effects of African culture on stigmas, based on their traditions, practices, and beliefs. They primarily are:

  • Link to Spirituality & Religion
  • Traditional Healing & Lack of Trust in Western Medicine
  • Perception of Mental Illness as Weakness or "Madness"

Link to Spirituality & Religion

In many African cultures, mental health issues are often understood through a spiritual or religious lens. Commonly, mental illness may be attributed to spiritual causes, such as possession by spirits, ancestral curses, or punishment for moral wrongdoing. This can lead to stigma, as individuals may be seen as morally or spiritually deficient, rather than suffering from a medical condition. The strong role of religion in African societies means that people might turn to religious leaders or traditional healers for diagnosis and treatment, which can either help or hinder access to professional mental health care.

Traditional Healing and Lack of Trust in Western Medicine

Traditional African healing practices, including the use of herbal remedies, rituals, and divination, are often the first line of treatment for mental health problems. Some communities may be skeptical or distrustful of modern psychiatric care, associating it with Western or colonial influences. This can lead to reluctance in seeking help from mental health professionals, contributing to the stigmatization of formal mental health care as unnecessary or culturally inappropriate.

Perception of Mental Illness as Weakness or "Madness"

In many African societies, mental health problems may be associated with weakness or a loss of control over one's behavior. Terms like "madness" or "insanity" are sometimes used to describe mental health issues, and the person affected may be viewed as someone who has lost their dignity or honor. This perception can result in shame and secrecy surrounding mental health issues, and people may avoid seeking help to avoid being labeled as "crazy." In some cultures, an individual with mental health problems might be hidden from the community to avoid bringing shame to the family.

Gender and Mental Health

The stigma surrounding mental health can also be gendered. In many African cultures, women may face particular challenges, as mental health issues can be seen as a sign of instability, particularly for women in caregiving roles. Depression, anxiety, and other disorders might be overlooked or dismissed as mere emotional reactions to gendered experiences like childbearing, marriage, or poverty. Women who suffer from conditions like post-partum depression or gender-based violence-related trauma might be less likely to receive proper care due to the stigma surrounding these issues.

Community and Family Pressure

African cultures often place a high value on family and community cohesion. Mental illness can be seen as a disruption to the social order, which can lead to isolation or rejection of the individual affected. The need to maintain family honor may overshadow the need for mental health treatment, leading to silence or denial. Families may be reluctant to talk openly about mental illness, and a person experiencing mental health challenges might be pressured to "keep it inside" or "tough it out."

Lack of Awareness and Education

In many African countries, mental health awareness is low due to limited mental health education, which exacerbates stigma. This is particularly the case in rural areas, where traditional beliefs and practices still dominate. The lack of mental health professionals, inadequate infrastructure, and limited access to treatment make it difficult for individuals to get the help they need, further entrenching negative perceptions about mental illness.

Colonial History and Mental Health

The legacy of colonialism has also played a role in shaping attitudes toward mental health in African cultures. Colonial governments often imposed Western systems of mental health care, which were sometimes used as tools of social control, labeling indigenous practices or those who resisted colonial authority as "mad." This historical association between mental health care and colonial oppression may contribute to ongoing reluctance to embrace modern psychiatric practices.

The Role of Social Media and Urbanization

In urban areas, particularly among younger generations, social media and increased global awareness of mental health issues are helping to reduce stigma. Many young people are more open to discussing mental health and seeking professional care, influenced by global mental health movements. However, in more traditional communities, these changing attitudes may clash with older generations' views, creating generational divides over the issue.

Mental Health in the Context of War, Poverty, and Trauma

Many African countries have experienced conflict, war, and widespread poverty, which have deep psychological impacts on the population. However, due to stigma, trauma and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) are often underdiagnosed or ignored. The societal pressure to endure hardship and to remain strong for the community or family can prevent individuals from seeking help, as mental health issues are seen as signs of personal failure or weakness.

The Role of African Diasporas

In African diaspora communities, attitudes toward mental health may shift due to exposure to different cultural practices and attitudes in the countries they migrate to. In some cases, people may bring with them the same stigmas and taboos, while in other cases, they may embrace new approaches to mental health care.

Researchers working with Harold Neighbors, PhD, University of Michigan, studied the interactions between culture, race, and ethnicity with depressive symptoms among a subset of participants from the National Survey of American Life, comprising 3,438 African Americans, Caribbean Americans, and white Americans. They evaluated social, group, and individual characteristics related to behavioral responses (such as coping strategies) to life stressors, group and personal identity, ideology, and beliefs about racial relations, and how these factors intersected with symptoms of depression. African Americans in this study did not show a significant relationship between depressive symptoms and high-effort coping strategies, while Caribbean Blacks and white Americans experienced increasing symptoms of depression linked to increasingly high-effort coping, in relationship to other beliefs and values.

The stigma surrounding mental health in African cultures is complex and shaped by a combination of traditional beliefs, religious views, social pressures, and historical contexts. While attitudes are slowly changing, particularly with younger generations, significant challenges remain in addressing mental health issues openly and effectively. Education, increased access to mental health services, and a shift towards recognizing mental illness as a medical condition rather than a moral or spiritual failing are key to reducing stigma in African societies.
